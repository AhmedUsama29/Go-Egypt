Woven Creations

An ancient Egyptian sign resembling a loop of rope, symbolizing protection and safety. It was carved on tombs and temples, used as an amulet, and often shown in the hands of protective deities to guard people in life and the afterlife.

Shen Ring

An ancient Egyptian symbol of eternity, protection, and completeness. Shaped as a loop of rope with a straight base, it represented the idea of encircling and safeguarding. Deities often held it around royal names or sacred objects, signifying divine protection and everlasting stability.

In ancient Egypt, a cartouche was an oval or elongated loop that enclosed and protected a royal name or title. It symbolized eternity and safeguarded the king‚Äôs identity, appearing on monuments, jewelry, and tomb walls to ensure the ruler‚Äôs name and spirit would live on forever.

Bag or Pouch

The bag or pouch symbol represents carrying, storage, and provision in ancient Egypt. It was often associated with offerings, treasures, or valuable items kept safe for the gods or the deceased. As a hieroglyph, it reflects themes of preservation, protection, and material abundance.

Human Form Symbols

Eye of Ra

A powerful emblem in ancient Egypt that symbolized the sun‚Äôs strength and the supreme authority of Ra. Associated with fierce goddesses such as Sekhmet and Hathor, it signified protection by force, defending the pharaoh and defeating chaos to preserve balance in the universe.

Eye of Horus (Wedjat)

An ancient Egyptian symbol of protection, healing, and well-being. Each part of the eye represents a sense, together symbolizing awareness, balance, and divine protection.

Fingers

The finger hieroglyph represents touch, action, and human precision. It was often used in words related to feeling, pointing, or performing tasks by hand. This symbol highlights the ancient Egyptian appreciation for skill, sensitivity, and the power of human action in daily life and ritual practice.

The pair of ears symbolizes hearing, awareness, and divine attention. It represents the gods‚Äô ability to listen to prayers and respond to the voices of the people. This symbol reflects the ancient Egyptian belief that the deities were always attentive to human words and devotion.

Kneeling man

The kneeling man represents worship, respect, and humility. It was often used to show a person offering prayers or presenting gifts to the gods. This posture reflects devotion, obedience, and the deep spiritual relationship between humans and the divine in ancient Egyptian culture.

Invertebrates & Insects

Scarab (Kheper)

In ancient Egypt, the scarab beetle symbolized rebirth, protection, and divine power. Associated with the god Kheper, it was used in amulets and royal rituals to protect pharaohs and represent the eternal cycle of life.

Scorpion

The scorpion symbolizes protection, healing, and power in ancient Egypt. It is closely associated with the goddess Serqet, who guarded the living and the dead from venom and harm. The symbol represents both danger and defense, reflecting the dual nature of the scorpion as a creature capable of both death and protection.

Snake

The snake represents power, protection, and divine energy in ancient Egypt. It was seen as both a guardian and a symbol of danger. Often linked to the cobra and the goddess Wadjet, the snake embodied royal authority and magical defense, guarding the pharaohs and sacred spaces from evil.

Locust

The locust symbolizes destruction, swarming, and natural power in ancient Egypt. It was associated with plagues and the forces of chaos that could devastate crops and life. Despite its negative meaning, the symbol also reminded people of the balance between nature‚Äôs creation and destruction.

Spider

The spider represents creativity, skill, and patience in ancient Egypt. It was associated with weaving and the careful crafting of webs, symbolizing order and creation. The spider‚Äôs ability to build intricate patterns reflected the Egyptians‚Äô respect for artistry and the interconnected nature of life.

Birds

Vulture (Nekhbet)

An ancient Egyptian goddess depicted as a vulture, symbolizing protection, motherhood, and royal authority. She guarded Upper Egypt and the pharaoh, often shown spreading her wings over kings, queens, and newborns to keep them safe.

Falcon (Horus)

The falcon symbolizes kingship, protection, and divine power in ancient Egypt. It represents the sky god Horus, one of the most important deities associated with the pharaoh. The sharp vision and soaring flight of the falcon reflected strength, clarity, and royal authority, linking earthly rulers with the gods of the heavens.

Heron

The heron, often associated with the god Thoth, symbolizes wisdom, knowledge, and the ability to navigate between the worlds of the living and the dead. Its graceful presence along the Nile made it a powerful symbol of life and fertility, as well as a guide for souls in the afterlife.

Swallow

The swallow symbolizes protection, love, and the arrival of spring in ancient Egypt. Its migratory nature and return to the same nesting sites each year made it a powerful symbol of loyalty and renewal. Swallows were often depicted in tombs to ensure safe passage to the afterlife.

Duck

The duck symbolizes fertility, motherhood, and the nurturing aspects of life in ancient Egypt. Often associated with the goddess Isis, ducks were seen as protectors of the young and were commonly depicted in scenes of family and domesticity.

Trees & Plants

Lotus

A sacred flower in ancient Egypt symbolizing creation, purity, rebirth, and the rising sun. Its daily cycle of closing at night and opening at dawn linked it to life, renewal, and the afterlife, and it appeared widely in art, temples, jewelry, and myths about the sun god‚Äôs birth.

Wheat & Barley

For the ancient Egyptians, these grains meant life and abundance. Wheat made their bread, and barley brewed their beer, symbols of prosperity and the blessings of Osiris, the god of farming and renewal.

Palm Tree

In ancient Egypt, the palm tree symbolized life, fertility, and abundance. Its leaves stood for victory and renewal, and its fruit offered nourishment. Palms often appeared in art and temple carvings as signs of growth, prosperity, and the promise of paradise.

Vine Branch

In ancient Egypt, grape branches symbolized fertility, abundance, and a thriving life. They appeared in art, tombs, and celebrations, representing growth, nourishment, and the blessings of nature.

Reed Bundle

In ancient Egypt, a bundle of reeds symbolized unity, strength, and order. It was used practically to make mats, baskets, ropes, and writing brushes. Symbolically, it represented many parts coming together to form a strong whole, as well as stability and support in life and governance.

Date Palm

In ancient Egypt, the date palm symbolized longevity, prosperity, and regeneration. It appeared in art, architecture, and tomb decorations, representing life, sustenance, and the enduring blessings of nature.

Ancient Egyptian Deities

Amun

Known as ‚ÄúThe Hidden One,‚Äù Amun was a powerful god of creation and hidden strength. Originating from Thebes, he became a symbol of the unseen force behind all life and later merged with Ra as Amun-Ra, the supreme god of the sun and kingship.

Ra

The sun god and one of the most important deities in ancient Egypt. Ra represented light, warmth, and life itself, traveling across the sky each day and through the underworld at night to be reborn at dawn. He symbolized creation, power, and the eternal cycle of life and renewal.

Osiris

The god of the afterlife, resurrection, and fertility, Osiris was one of the most important deities in ancient Egypt. He ruled the underworld and symbolized renewal, justice, and eternal life. After being killed by his brother Set and revived by Isis, Osiris became a symbol of rebirth and the triumph of life over death.

Isis

The goddess of magic, healing, and motherhood, Isis was one of the most beloved deities in ancient Egypt. She represented love, protection, and devotion, utilizing her magic to revive Osiris and safeguard their son, Horus. Isis symbolized the ideal mother and wife, as well as the power of compassion and eternal life.

Horus

The falcon-headed god of the sky, kingship, and protection, Horus symbolized divine power and victory. He was the son of Isis and Osiris, known for avenging his father‚Äôs death and restoring order to Egypt. As the living embodiment of the pharaoh, Horus represented justice, strength, and the eternal balance between light and darkness.

Hathor

The goddess of love, beauty, music, and joy, Hathor was a nurturing figure who welcomed souls into the afterlife. Often depicted as a cow or a woman with cow horns and a sun disk, she represented motherhood, harmony, and the warmth of the sun. Hathor embodied happiness, femininity, and the power of love that sustains life.

Anubis

The jackal-headed god of mummification and the afterlife, Anubis guided souls through the journey of death and protected the process of embalming. He watched over tombs and performed the weighing of the heart to judge purity. Anubis symbolized protection, guidance, and the sacred balance between life and death.

Thoth

The god of wisdom, writing, and knowledge, Thoth was believed to have invented hieroglyphs and served as the scribe of the gods. Often depicted with the head of an ibis, he maintained the balance of the universe and recorded the results of the judgment of souls. Thoth symbolized intelligence, truth, and the power of words.

Khnum

The ram-headed god of creation and fertility, Khnum was believed to have shaped humans on his potter‚Äôs wheel from the clay of the Nile. He controlled the waters and the annual flooding that brought life to Egypt. Khnum symbolized creativity, renewal, and the divine force that gives form and sustains life.

Sobek

The crocodile-headed god of strength, protection, and the Nile, Sobek embodied both the river‚Äôs life-giving power and its dangerous nature. He was seen as a fierce protector of the pharaoh and a symbol of fertility, courage, and military might. Sobek represented the balance between creation and destruction within nature.

Sekhmet

The lioness-headed goddess of war, power, and healing, Sekhmet embodied fierce strength and divine justice. She was believed to protect the pharaoh in battle and to bring both destruction and restoration. As a symbol of fiery energy and balance, Sekhmet represented the dual nature of wrath and healing in maintaining cosmic order.

Set

The god of chaos, storms, and the desert, Set represented strength, unpredictability, and disorder. Though often seen as the rival of Osiris and Horus, he also protected the sun god Ra on his nightly journey through the underworld. Set symbolized both destruction and the raw power necessary for balance in the universe.
