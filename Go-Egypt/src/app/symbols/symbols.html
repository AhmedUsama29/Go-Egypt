Symbols of Ancient Egypt

Discover the rich symbolism of Ancient Egypt, where every hieroglyph and icon carried deep meaning. These symbols connected the physical and spiritual worlds, representing the complex beliefs of one of history's most fascinating civilizations.

Explore our collection of the most significant Egyptian symbols and their meanings, from the powerful Ankh to the protective Eye of Horus.

Life & Power

Ankh

An ancient Egyptian sign shaped like a cross with a loop on top. Known as the “key of life,” it symbolized vitality and everlasting life, often shown in the hands of gods as they offered the gift of living energy.

Was Sceptre

Is a long, elegant staff from ancient Egypt, topped with a creature’s head and split at the base. It stood for strength, good fortune, and divine authority, and was a favorite symbol of gods and pharaohs.

Sekhem Scepter

An ancient Egyptian ceremonial staff symbolizing authority, strength, and divine power. Often shown in the hands of gods or high officials, it represented control, stability, and the vital force that sustains life.

Crook & Flail

Ancient Egyptian symbols of royal power and authority. The crook represents guidance and protection, while the flail symbolizes strength and order, showing the pharaoh’s role as both caretaker and ruler.

Eye of Ra

A powerful emblem in ancient Egypt that symbolized the sun’s strength and the supreme authority of Ra. Associated with fierce goddesses such as Sekhmet and Hathor, it signified protection by force, defending the pharaoh and defeating chaos to preserve balance in the universe.

Protection & Healing

Scarab (Kheper)

In ancient Egypt, the scarab beetle symbolized rebirth, protection, and divine power. Associated with the god Kheper, it was used in amulets and royal rituals to protect pharaohs and represent the eternal cycle of life.

Eye of Horus (Wedjat)

An ancient Egyptian symbol of protection, healing, and well-being. Each part of the eye represents a sense, together symbolizing awareness, balance, and divine protection.

Sa Symbol

An ancient Egyptian sign resembling a loop of rope, symbolizing protection and safety. It was carved on tombs and temples, used as an amulet, and often shown in the hands of protective deities to guard people in life and the afterlife.

Vulture (Nekhbet)

An ancient Egyptian goddess depicted as a vulture, symbolizing protection, motherhood, and royal authority. She guarded Upper Egypt and the pharaoh, often shown spreading her wings over kings, queens, and newborns to keep them safe.

Winged Sun

An ancient Egyptian symbol showing a solar disk flanked by falcon wings and cobras, representing protection, royal authority, and the sun’s power. It decorated temples and sacred objects, signifying victory and divine guardianship over people and places.

Shen Ring

An ancient Egyptian symbol of eternity, protection, and completeness. Shaped as a loop of rope with a straight base, it represented the idea of encircling and safeguarding. Deities often held it around royal names or sacred objects, signifying divine protection and everlasting stability.

Cats

To the ancient Egyptians, cats were more than pets; they were protectors and bringers of good luck. Believed to guard homes from pests and bad spirits, they belonged to Bastet, the gentle goddess of home and joy. Loved and respected, cats were often mummified so they could be honored even after death.

Cartouche

In ancient Egypt, a cartouche was an oval or elongated loop that enclosed and protected a royal name or title. It symbolized eternity and safeguarded the king’s identity, appearing on monuments, jewelry, and tomb walls to ensure the ruler’s name and spirit would live on forever.

Nature & Universe

Lotus

A sacred flower in ancient Egypt symbolizing creation, purity, rebirth, and the rising sun. Its daily cycle of closing at night and opening at dawn linked it to life, renewal, and the afterlife, and it appeared widely in art, temples, jewelry, and myths about the sun god’s birth.

Papyrus

A tall reed from the Nile Delta symbolizing fertility, life, and prosperity. The lotus represented the unification of Upper and Lower Egypt, known as the “Two Lands,” and its stems were used to make papyrus sheets, the earliest form of paper for writing, art, and records.

Wheat & Barley

For the ancient Egyptians, these grains meant life and abundance. Wheat made their bread, and barley brewed their beer, symbols of prosperity and the blessings of Osiris, the god of farming and renewal.

Palm Tree

In ancient Egypt, the palm tree symbolized life, fertility, and abundance. Its leaves stood for victory and renewal, and its fruit offered nourishment. Palms often appeared in art and temple carvings as signs of growth, prosperity, and the promise of paradise.

Sycamore Fig

In ancient Egypt, this “Tree of Life” stood for nourishment, protection, and eternity. Tied to goddesses like Hathor, Nut, and Isis, it was thought to shelter souls. Planted near tombs, its wood served for coffins and its fruit for food, symbolizing renewal and lasting care.

Royal & Divine Insignia

Feather of Ma’at

Feather of Ma’at: To the ancient Egyptians, this feather stood for truth, fairness, and balance in the world. In the afterlife, they believed a person’s heart was weighed against it; if the heart was as light as a feather, the soul was pure and ready for eternal peace.

White Crown (Hedjet)

A royal headdress of ancient Egypt, part of the Royal & Divine Insignia, it signified enduring rule over Upper Egypt. Its tall white form reflected stability, guidance, and a subtle link between royal power and higher order, later pairing with the Red Crown to form the Double Crown of unity.

Red Crown (Deshret)

A royal headdress of ancient Egypt, part of the Royal & Divine Insignia, it marked enduring rule over Lower Egypt. Its flat red form reflected strength, protection, and a link between earthly power and a higher order, later pairing with the White Crown to create the Double Crown of unity.

Double Crown (Pschent)

A royal headdress of ancient Egypt, part of the Royal & Divine Insignia, symbolizing the union of Upper and Lower Egypt. Blending the White and Red Crowns, it represented balance, strength, and the pharaoh’s rule over a united land.

Blue Crown (Khepresh)

A royal headdress of ancient Egypt, part of the Royal & Divine Insignia, symbolizing the union of Upper and Lower Egypt. Blending the White and Red Crowns, it represented balance, strength, and the pharaoh’s rule over a united land.
